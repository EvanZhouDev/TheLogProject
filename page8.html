Euler's Number and Natural Logarithms

Euler's number is an irrational constant similar to the trusty value \(\pi\) (pi). Its value is approximated to be \(2.71828\). The actual number itself appears in nature many times, and is sometimes categorized with exponential growth. However, Euler's number is also used when taking when taking a logarithm with base \(e\). As with all important expressions, \(\log_e x\) has been abstracted and given its own name: The natural logarithm, or \(\ln x\).

\(\log_e x = \ln x\)

When you are expressing a natural logarithm, you do not provide the base—it is always \(e\). Here is a visualization of the graph of \(f(x)=\ln x\)

Euler's Number and Logarithms

Evaluate these logarithms. Do not round:

\(e^{\ln 4}\)

When looking at these numbers, you might think "If \(e\) is irrational, then how do I evaluate without rounding? " This is the time to employ our logarithm properties that we learned a while back. We can use the inverse property to simplify this. Let's rewrite the problem slightly: \(e^{\log_e 4}\). Do you see how we can cancel? The \(e\) and the logarithm of base \(e\) cancel to just leave 4. the answer would be 4.

\(\ln e^{100}\)

Similarly with this problem, we can use the inverse property to simplify. Let's rewrite again: \(\log_e e^{100}\). The logarithm of base \(e\) and the \(e\) itself cancel out, leaving our answer, \(100\).

\({\ln 8 \over \ln 2}\)

Keeping in mind that the natural logarithm is just a logarithm of base \(e\), the Change of Base formula comes in handy here. We can rewrite this as \({\log_e 8 \over \log_e 2}\), or \(\log_2 8\). When we evaluate, we get the answer, 3.
